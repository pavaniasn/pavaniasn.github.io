Who Are You Comparing Yourself To?

The other day, a colleague of mine mentioned Malcolm Gladwell in passing — something about how success is shaped by factors beyond talent, beyond grit, beyond the effort we put in. That's a familiar Gladwell argument, and I'd nodded along to it before. But it got me curious about his other work, and that curiosity led me to a video of him speaking at Google Zeitgeist where he says, plainly: "If you want to get a science and math degree, don't go to Harvard."

I stopped. Being a Harvard alum myself, I didn't know whether to laugh or feel defensive.

So I kept reading. Gladwell was pointing to something called the Big Fish, Little Pond effect — the idea that your experience of your own ability is shaped not by how capable you actually are, but by who you are standing next to. A student who might have thrived and stayed in science at a mid-tier college walks into Harvard, looks around, feels like the least capable person in the room, and quietly stops believing they belong there. Not because they weren't smart. Because of the pond they were in.

That led me to Samuel Stouffer, a researcher who studied American soldiers during World War II. He wanted to know which soldiers felt most satisfied — most like the system was working for them. His hypothesis was obvious: soldiers in units with higher promotion rates must be happier. More opportunity, more morale. Simple.

The results were the opposite. Soldiers in high-promotion units felt worse. Because in those units, they watched peers advance every single day. The comparison was constant, unavoidable, and it made those who hadn't been promoted yet feel like they were falling behind. Meanwhile, in units where promotions were rare, nobody felt left behind — because nobody was ahead of them to feel bad about.

Stouffer called this relative deprivation. And I'd never heard of it before that afternoon.

That led me to Leon Festinger and his Social Comparison Theory — the observation that we have no internal compass for measuring our own worth. We look sideways. We find someone close to our level and use them as the reference point. I am learning there is this much to discover just by following one curious thread. That itself felt worth sitting with.

But what surprised me more was how quickly all of this brought me back to my mother.

Her first question, whenever I mentioned making a new friend growing up, was always: "Is she a topper in your class? Is he doing well?" I used to think she was judging my friendships. Now I think she understood something intuitively that Stouffer and Festinger eventually put names to — that the company you keep shapes how you see yourself, and what you reach for.

I don't know if that was her wiring me, or something I built on my own from there. But somewhere along the way, I started keeping company with people I genuinely look up to — people who are doing something better than me, psychologically, emotionally, intellectually. It doesn't have to be the same dimension every time. Just: someone I can learn from by proximity.

Which means I have probably always been in a state of relative deprivation, if we're using the term correctly. I am usually the person in the room who feels like the least accomplished one there.

I used to read that as an inferiority complex. Now I'm less sure.

What was interesting about taking all of this to the students I was speaking to — a room full of interns — was realizing that I had been living some version of this all along, without the vocabulary. I had never heard of Stouffer or Festinger before, never known the terms relative deprivation or social comparison theory. And yet something in me had already been practicing what they describe — through instinct, or upbringing, or something in between. The theories gave me a name for what I was already doing. That's what good frameworks do, I think.

The question I keep sitting with though: comparison is so deeply human. It is wired into us. So when does it tip? When does something so natural start curdling into envy, or resentment, or something that makes us smaller rather than larger? I don't have an answer. But I think knowing that the comparison is happening — and choosing, even partly, what you do with it — is where something shifts.

On Luck, Grace, and the Work in Between

Many times, when I've asked people how they got to where they are, they've walked me through the work, the hours, the decisions, the risks. But just as many times, I've heard: "I was lucky. God's grace. Right place, right time."

I came across a line by Paul Graham recently that stayed with me: "Make yourself a big target for luck." His argument is that curiosity expands your surface area. Read, meet people, ask questions, try things, and luck has more places to land.

I took that idea into a morning talk with a thousand students. And somewhere in that room, a thought surfaced that I hadn't planned.

We say "right place, right time, right people," but then we call it luck. Why? Because we can't draw the straight line backward. The chain is too long, too non-linear, too full of small invisible decisions. So we collapse it. We call it luck because it's easier.

But here's what that collapsing costs us: it erases our own role in getting there. The book we read. The room we chose to walk into. The risk we took when we didn't have to.

I don't disagree with the luck and grace framing. I say it myself. I am a deeply spiritual person, and I have genuinely attributed so much of my own trajectory to divine grace, the people I met, the opportunities that appeared, the doors that opened when I wasn't even knocking. I mean that sincerely, not as a figure of speech.

But here is the question I keep sitting with: would those same people, those same chance encounters, have shaped my life the way they did if I hadn't shown up the way I did? If I hadn't read what I read, asked what I asked, stayed in rooms I was intimidated by?

I don't know. And I'm not sure I can know.

What I do notice is this: when we reach for "luck" or "grace" to explain an outcome, there is a quiet risk. Not that we're wrong, but that we erase, without meaning to, the effort that created the conditions for that grace to land.

And then the bigger question, the one I don't have an answer to: does this even play out the same way for everyone? Does how much we attribute to luck versus effort depend on where we come from, our culture, our class, our gender, where we sit in the social hierarchy? Is "I worked hard and made it happen" a narrative more available to some than others? And is "it was God's grace" sometimes the most honest thing a person can say, because the structural odds were so stacked that effort alone couldn't have been enough?

I'm genuinely asking. I don't have a position to defend here. Just a question I find myself unable to leave alone.

Paul Graham says make yourself a big target. I'd add: and when luck finds you, be honest about what you also did to be standing there. And maybe, honest too about whether that path was equally available to begin with.
