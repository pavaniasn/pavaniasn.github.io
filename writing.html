Make Yourself a Big Target

Yesterday I came across a line by Paul Graham: "Make yourself a big target for luck." His argument is that curiosity expands your surface area, read, meet people, ask questions, try things, and luck has more places to land.

I took that into a morning talk with a thousand students. And somewhere in that room, a thought surfaced that I hadn't planned.

We say "right place, right time, right people," but then we call it luck. Why? Because we can't draw the straight line backward. The chain is too long, too non-linear, too full of small invisible decisions. So we collapse it. We call it luck because it's easier.

But here's what that collapsing costs us: it erases our own role in getting there. The book we read. The room we chose to walk into. The risk we took when we didn't have to.

I'm not arguing luck doesn't exist. I'm saying that every time we reach for that word too quickly, we hand away credit that was at least partly ours to keep.

Paul Graham says make yourself a big target. I'd add: and when luck finds you, be honest about what you also did to be standing there.
